Reflections of a Not-Quite-First-Rate Writer

Every once in a while—really every other day—I'm afflicted with a suddenly entrenched and blistering feeling of incompetence of the literary variety. Frustration boils over to a point of intellectual dormancy, and my mind is rendered out of sorts.

In my mind's eye there is a writer, reader and perpetual student less indecisive, insecure and scatterbrained than I. She remains tenaciously tunnel focused, intellectually robust and experienced, yet contrasts the routinized humdrum of my life with an existence so sweetly adorned with a sort of musical quality it's almost tragic; tuned perfectly, beautifully, to something akin to a Bill Evans piece. Remaining for the most part in the recesses of my mind, she silently emerges in full form at these sordid moments of painfully abject insecurity, reminding me of all my rough edges, my flimsiness and my inexperience. In a fit of confoundedness bordering on the imbecilic, I allow my despondency—and what I consider to be her undeniable supremacy over me—to (ever imperceptibly) stutter my progression.

I regard my skepticism to be in some ways a weakness of my being. I presume that it is a reflexive mechanism I have developed in response to what I commonly perceive to be the paralyzing, ubiquitous fog of dogmatism. I wish to not transform this habitual response into anything approximating a vain contrarianism, or into aimless theatrics of conjecture. It's not so much that I am a harshly judgmental person as I am an ever critical one (many a time to my own chagrin); subject to much of this critique being those positions and modalities that can best be described as status quo. Another common subject of my criticisms is of course myself. But I don't wish to write much of my person, not explicitly at least.

In his 1955 essay collection, Notes of a Native Son, Baldwin writes: "One writes out of one thing only—one's own experience. Everything depends on how relentlessly one forces from this experience the last drop, sweet or bitter, it can possibly give. This is the only real concern of the artist, to recreate out of the disorder of life that order which is art." I became increasingly prepared as time passed, to do the work of persistently forcing whatever it is from my experiences that a good writer ought to use, but I'm afraid that despite this readiness, my experiences turned out to have been somewhat banal. How does one remedy a lack of experiences (or good experiences?), which is, really, a lack of knowledge? I suppose I took to reading to solve this very dilemma, and a defining theme of my life since appears to have become one of perpetual renovation; mainly renovations, and reconstructions, of the intellectual sort. My ever expanding list of literary, scholarly, and journalistic reads ensures my commitment, in effect, to this life of perpetual renovation, as does the aforementioned skepticism constantly presiding over my thoughts. I reflect upon these observations and I think: this is possibly part and parcel of what Baldwin described as the kinetic, potential-filled disorder of the writer's life. And I'm certainly a little relieved for that.

Writing a full-fledged autobiography is perhaps above my age bracket, so I should refrain from prattling on any longer. Thank you for paying my thoughts a visit and, please, feel free reach out to me via email if you would like.
